A spoonie is a person living with limited daily energy often due to chronic illness or nervous system disorders. People who use the term do so as a self-identifier and a shorthand for a shared experience. The word grew out of Spoon Theory, and a spoonie is someone who identifies as a member of the community.

> - [Read the full explainer on Spoon Theory](/what-is-spoon-theory)
{: .callout-resources}

## Who Calls Themselves a Spoonie?

The most common spoonies are people living with chronic illness. That covers a long list of conditions, including fibromyalgia, ME/CFS, lupus, multiple sclerosis, EDS, POTS, long COVID, Lyme disease, autoimmune diseases, thyroid disease, diabetes, anemia, and many more. 

Mental health conditions like depression, anxiety, and complex PTSD belong here too. So does neurodivergence, including autism and ADHD. Anything that puts a real limit on how much energy you have. 

The term has also expanded beyond formal diagnoses. The chronic illness community has been generous about who uses the term, and the everyday reality is that lots of people live with chronic low energy from causes that aren't strictly illness. 

People who have considered themselves part of the spoonie community include:

- Cancer survivors 
- People recovering from major surgery or injury
- New parents of medically complex babies
- People in clinical burnout
- People living with complex trauma
- People navigating high-stress poverty

The common thread isn't what's wrong on paper. It's that the person is operating with less daily energy than the world expects them to have, and they have to plan around it. 

## You Might Be a Spoonie If…

You don't need a diagnosis to recognize yourself in this list. Most people in the community had a long stretch of suspecting they were a spoonie before any doctor confirmed it.

- You've had to choose between a shower and making dinner
- A single errand can wipe you out for the rest of the day
- You've cancelled plans because your body said no, then you felt guilty for the rest of the day
- "Resting" doesn't restore you the way it seems to for healthy people
- You've said "I just need to lie down" more times than you can count

## Why We Use This Word

Calling yourself a spoonie does three things at once.

1. **It gives the experience context**. There is a real difference between "I'm tired" and "I'm out of spoons," and having a word that captures the difference matters. It signals that what you're going through is not the normal tiredness everyone has.

2. **It's shorthand**. Instead of explaining a chronic condition, your pacing strategy, and your boundary for the day, you can just say you're low on spoons. A stranger may not get it. Another spoonie will.

3. **It's a way to find your people**. Searching the word "spoonie" on Instagram, TikTok, Pinterest, or anywhere else turns up a community that doesn't need the explanation. There's a real relief in finding people who already understand.

## A Note on Community Etiquette

Spoonie is meant for people whose daily energy is _genuinely_ limited by illness, mental health, caregiving, recovery, or other persistent conditions. It is generally frowned upon for non-spoonies to use the framework to describe normal tiredness ("I'm heading to bed, I'm all out of spoons"), full schedules ("none of us have the spoons to start the project"), or frustration with someone's behavior ("I don't have the spoons for your attitude").

This isn't gatekeeping for the sake of it. It's about protecting the meaning of a word that helps the people who actually need it to be understood.
