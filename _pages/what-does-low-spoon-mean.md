"Low spoon" is the term spoonies use to describe a day when our daily energy is running low or already gone. The phrase comes from Spoon Theory, the framework [Christine Miserandino created in 2003](https://web.archive.org/web/20231001120000/https://butyoudontlooksick.com/articles/written-by-christine/the-spoon-theory/) to explain limited daily energy.

## The Spoon Vocabulary
The spoonie community uses a small set of phrases to talk about energy levels, and they all mean slightly different things.

- **Low spoon.** We still have energy, but we can feel it running out. The day is going to require tough decisions about what gets done and what doesn't.
- **No spoons.** We're done. The energy is gone. Rest and recovery is the only remaining option.
- **Out of spoons.** Same as no spoons, often used in past tense at the end of a day to explain why something didn't happen.
- **Spoon count.** How many spoons we woke up with that day. Some of us use literal numbers ("I started with maybe four spoons"). Others use it loosely.
- **Borrowing from tomorrow.** Pushing through when we're out, which always works in the moment and always costs us the next day.

## What Being Low Spoon Feels Like

For people who haven't experienced it, "low spoon" often gets translated as "tired." That's not quite right. Tiredness is a symptom. Low spoon is a state.

What it actually feels like, for many of us:

- The body gets heavy. Standing up requires enormous energy.
- Brain fog rolls in. The words for things go missing. Sentences trail off.
- Decisions get harder. Picking what to eat can take twenty minutes.
- Starting anything feels disproportionate to the actual task. Loading the dishwasher might as well be moving furniture.
- Sound, light, and conversation start to feel louder than they did an hour ago.
- You start to worry about things left undone and how you will feel tomorrow.

Not every low-spoon day looks the same. Different conditions produce different results. The shared thread is that being low spoons is a whole-body, whole-brain experience. It's not just being tired. It's being tired plus a real, measurable limit on what our bodies and minds are capable of.

## How Low Spoon Is Different From Being Tired

Have you ever had a laptop where the battery doesn't hold a charge anymore? The kind where you can use it fine when it's plugged in, but the second you unplug, the percentage drops way too fast. Maybe it doesn't even hit 100% on the charger anymore. You learn to plan your day around outlets.

That's a pretty accurate picture of what being a spoonie feels like. Our bodies are the laptop. Rest, sleep, food, pacing are all the things that should recharge our battery. But the charger is faulty. A full night of sleep doesn't get us all the way to 100%. A normal day drains us faster than it should. We're always thinking about our energy levels, wondering when they will run out.

## What We Do on Low Spoon Days

The most useful thing we can do on a low-spoon day is decide what to skip before our brains stop being able to decide anything at all. Pre-made rules save spoons:

- **The easy meal.** Cereal for dinner is fine. 
- **Physical accomodations.** We find creative ways to reduce the spoon cost. For example, sitting through a shower costs roughly half the spoons of standing through one. 
- **The no-guilt cancel.** Plans get cancelled, sometimes last minute.
- **The cut corner.** The bed doesn't always have to get made. 
- **The water and the snack.** Dehydration and low blood sugar make low spoons worse for almost everyone.
- **The lie-down.** Laying down rather than sitting is essential to restoring or conserving energy.

The goal of a low-spoon day is to end it without going into spoon debt for the next day.

## When We're Truly Out of Spoons

On the days when there are no spoons left, the question stops being "what should I do" and becomes "what am I allowed to skip."

The honest answer is: most things. Dishes can wait. Laundry can wait. Most of the inbox can wait. A messy house is not a failure of character. 

A short permission list for the days when there's nothing left:

- We're allowed to eat the easiest possible thing
- We're allowed to skip the shower
- We're allowed to leave the dishes
- We're allowed to text instead of call
- We're allowed to cancel
- We're allowed to lie down for as long as our bodies need

If you're new to chronic illness, the hardest part of this won't be the resting. It will be giving yourself permission to rest.
