- q: "How much protein does a chronically ill person need?" 
  a: "Chronically ill people often need more protein than the general population because muscle loss is common with chronic conditions, especially when activity is limited. General research-backed guidance puts the range at 1.2 to 2.0 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day. For a 150-pound person, that's roughly 80 to 135 grams a day. Your doctor will give you the right number for your situation."

- q: "What are the easiest high-protein foods for low-spoon days?" 
  a: "The ones that require no cooking and minimal decisions. Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, jerky, hard-boiled eggs you bought already cooked at the grocery store, protein shakes, cheese sticks, edamame from a microwave-steam bag, and canned tuna. Stack two or three of these together and you have a real meal without ever turning on the stove."

- q: "Can you hit 95g of protein without cooking?" 
  a: "Yes, with intention. Stacking yogurt, cottage cheese, protein shakes, jerky, hemp hearts, and cheese can get a spoonie to 95g without ever using the stove. The trick is keeping these foods stocked so they are always an option, and giving yourself permission to use them every single day if that's what your body needs."

- q: "Do protein shakes count as real protein?" 
  a: "Yes. Protein shakes aren't a cheat, they're a tool. They were literally designed for people whose bodies need protein and can't easily get it from whole food. That is a spoonie. Aim for shakes with at least 20 grams of protein and ingredients you can pronounce. The right shake on the right day saves your nervous system from a cooking spiral."

- q: "How do you track protein when brain fog makes tracking impossible?" 
  a: "You don't track perfectly. You build a list of your usual foods with the protein amounts beside each one, and you keep that list visible (taped to the pantry door is more useful than buried in an app). Then you aim for a rough total at each meal, not a perfect daily count. The goal is intention, not arithmetic."

Having a chronic illness is not a permission slip to lay around and eat whatever we want. Far from it. As [spoonies](/who-is-a-spoonie/) we often have to eat even better than the average person to manage our conditions.

When my new doctor found out I had insulin resistance on top of everything else, she asked me to start eating 95 grams of protein a day. My very first thought was a spoonie thought. _How can I do this efficiently?_ I thought I'd just add a hard-boiled egg to my breakfast and call it good.

Until I found out a hard-boiled egg would only add 6 grams of protein to my day. Suddenly what seemed like a simple ask became a mountain to overcome.

## Why 95g Is Hard for a Spoonie

The mountain was cooking, one of the hardest spoonie tasks for me. So many high-protein meals require cooking from scratch, and cooking meat in particular can be complex. I don't have the spoons to do large batch meal prep, so I knew that was out.

The other task stressing me out was tracking. Spoonie brain fog is real. I knew for a fact that tracking protein at every meal was not a possibility. Maybe I could use the spoons to get started, but I wouldn't be able to sustain it.

> In my spoonie life, I have everything balanced. That means I don't have crashes as often anymore. I live by my routines. But it also means there isn't room for new things. If I add something, it comes at the expense of something else.

I knew to add more protein, I needed a strategy.

## **Science Behind Protein for Chronic Illness**

The 95g number wasn't pulled from thin air. [Research on protein needs in chronic illness](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11451713/) shows that chronically ill people often need significantly more protein than the general population to combat muscle loss and support recovery. 

If you want a broader overview, here's a [recent review](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11279393/) covering cardiovascular disease, diabetes, inflammation, and muscle preservation. 

## **My Protein Strategy as a Chronically Ill Person**

Here's what is working for me.

### Getting my building blocks
I made a list of foods and their protein amounts as my building blocks. Think of this list as your individual Legos that you can combine to create protein-rich meals.
- **Collagen for my coffee** (9g)
- **Cottage cheese** (13g)
- **Eggs** (6g per egg)
- **6oz of chicken** (45g)
- **Hemp hearts** (10g)
- **Greek yogurt** (15g)
- **Protein shake** (30g)
- **Can of tuna** (25g per can)
- **Protein chips** (10g)
- **Keto bread** (6g per slice)
- **Edamame** (17g)

Once I had my list, I printed it out and taped it to the inside of my pantry door as reference. There was no way I was going to remember this, and putting it on my phone means I need to know where my phone is, which is a struggle.

### Building my protein meals

Then I set a goal. I only eat two meals a day plus a snack, so I knew I needed to combine.

- **A Tuna Salad Sandwich.** Tuna + mayo + hemp hearts + keto bread makes a tuna salad sandwich with 47 grams of protein. Add in some protein chips for 10 extra grams and suddenly it didn't seem so hard.

- **A High-Protein Breakfast.** Greek yogurt + hemp hearts + a piece of keto toast + collagen coffee makes a 41-gram breakfast.

- **The No-Spoon Backup.** Protein shakes on the days I just can't are an essential part of my strategy. I'm still looking for the right protein shake, but in a pinch I'm using the Atkins shakes because I don't have to mix anything to make them happen.

## **Why I Refuse to Stress About Protein**

Stress makes me worse. Unhelpful internal pressure, guilt of unmet expectations, and stress of keeping up completely tank my nervous system. Avoiding cortisol spikes is one of my spoonie strategies, and I have to balance that with my need to get insulin resistance under control.

Here are some permissions I give myself:

- Hitting 95g is a goal, but I'm going to celebrate any day I get more protein in, no matter the number. It is still better than where I was.

- A rough mental count is fine as long as I'm making the effort to add more protein at every meal.

- Protein shakes are fine on the days I just can't, and when I find better-tasting, cleaner ones, I'll swap them out.

- I hug myself on the days I can't, because it usually means something else is going on and I don't have the spoons to do both. Kindness matters.
